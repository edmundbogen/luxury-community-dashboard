The New Economics of Private Clubs

Scarcity, Rising Fees and Generational Shifts

Back to Market Dashboard
By Edmund Bogen | The Edmund Bogen Team at Douglas Elliman
December 2025

Introduction

Private golf and social clubs have undergone a dramatic transformation in the past decade. Once a pleasant amenity, club membership has become a scarce asset that commands substantial capital contributions and confers significant real-estate advantages.

Demand has surged among younger, high-income households seeking resort-style amenities and a sense of community; supply remains limited because few new clubs are built and many existing clubs cap membership. This new dynamic has reshaped club economics—particularly in South Florida's ultra-premium communities—and has pushed home values higher in communities that offer immediate club access.

National and South Florida Fee Trends

A Nationwide Surge

Nationwide, median initiation fees climbed from around $29,000 in 2019 to more than $50,000 by 2022, a 72% increase in just three years. Golf Property Analysts estimated that entrance fees increased about 23% per year between 2020 and 2024, essentially tripling in five years.

More than half of private clubs now maintain waitlists, whereas only 25% did pre-pandemic. The typical waitlist contains around 70 names.

Top-Tier and Florida Examples

Fees at top clubs dwarf the national median. Arizona's Desert Mountain Club raised its full-golf initiation from $70,000 in early 2022 to $225,000 by 2025, while Estancia Club climbed from $100,000 in 2017 to $275,000 by late 2024.

Newer projects like the Apogee Club in Hobe Sound, Florida, plan regular initiation fees of $475,000–$600,000, and Panther National in Palm Beach Gardens will charge residents roughly $300,000, with non-residents paying $400,000.

Established Florida clubs are also escalating fees. Addison Reserve in Delray Beach now requires a $325,000 non-refundable joining fee for full-golf membership. Boca West Country Club increased its initiation fee to about $149,900 with annual base dues of $22,593.

St Andrews Country Club Insight: At St Andrews Country Club in Boca Raton, the capital contribution was roughly $300,000 in 2024, and club communications indicate it will rise to $450,000 in 2026; buyers must also pay about $50,000 to the property owners' association (POA), bringing the total cash requirement to $500,000.

Importantly, St Andrews' capital fee is non-refundable and membership is mandatory for homeowners, but there is no waitlist—new buyers receive immediate golf access upon closing.

By contrast, Woodfield Country Club (also in Boca Raton) caps full-golf membership at 425 and reportedly has a 7- to 10-year waitlist. Transferable memberships are scarce. This difference in access pushes some buyers who want immediate golf privileges toward St Andrews despite its higher up-front cost.

The Mechanics of Scarcity

Why Memberships Are Rare

Several forces underpin this scarcity:

  • Limited supply: There are about 3,800 private clubs today, down from 5,500 in the 1930s. New clubs require large tracts of land and face regulatory hurdles. Supply in South Florida is particularly constrained.
  • Surging demand: Golf boomed during the pandemic; the U.S. added 3.3 million new golfers since 2020. Remote work allows affluent households to spend more time at second homes, and favorable Florida tax laws attract out-of-state buyers.
  • Waitlists as a revenue tool: With demand exceeding supply, many clubs keep prospective members on lists for years, allowing them to raise fees without losing buyers. About 71% of clubs charging more than $90,000 in initiation fees maintain waitlists, and the lists often exceed 100 names.
  • Mandatory membership: In communities like St Andrews or Addison Reserve, buying a home requires purchasing a club membership. That creates a captive market where the club can raise fees knowing that home buyers have little choice.

How Waitlists Drive Real Estate

The scarcity of memberships also influences property values. At Silverleaf Club in Arizona, the $400,000 initiation fee is attached to a home; sellers can command a $1 million premium because buyers value jumping the waitlist. Similar dynamics occur in Florida.

Clubs that allow immediate membership through home ownership (St Andrews, Bears Club) often see stronger price appreciation than clubs where the buyer must sit on a waitlist.

Academic research confirms the real-estate effect. A Florida Atlantic University study of more than 10,000 transactions in Miami-Dade, Broward and Palm Beach counties found that homes adjacent to golf courses commanded an 8–12% premium. Real estate experts interviewed for the study agreed that golf courses increase home prices by providing green space and exclusivity. This premium applies whether or not the course is part of a mandatory-membership community, but it is magnified when membership is scarce.

Demographic and Lifestyle Shifts

The buyer profile for private clubs is younger and wealthier than in past decades. A report from Golf Life Navigators found that the average age of new private-club members fell from 58.6 in 2017 to 54.9 in 2020. A 2025 hospitality analysis noted that clubs successfully navigating this shift now see 60–70% of new members under age 45.

These younger members often come from technology, finance and creative industries and seek wellness, family-friendly programs, and remote-work facilities.

80% of prospective club members consider real estate availability when evaluating clubs.

Because club membership is increasingly integral to lifestyle rather than retirement, many buyers evaluate club communities before narrowing down home choices. Mandatory-membership communities may seem expensive, but their exclusivity, immediate access and substantial amenities make them attractive to buyers who value time and certainty over bargain shopping.

Conclusion: Scarcity and Exclusivity Drive Value

The economics of private clubs now resemble those of scarce luxury assets: limited supply, surging demand and a demographic shift toward younger, high-income buyers.

In South Florida, initiation fees have climbed into the mid-six figures, with St Andrews Country Club poised to raise its capital contribution to $450,000 plus a $50,000 POA fee by 2026. Meanwhile, clubs like Woodfield enforce long waitlists, underscoring the value of communities where membership is mandatory and immediate.

This scarcity not only supports higher club fees but also lifts home prices; properties adjacent to or within private clubs command notable premiums.

For buyers and real-estate professionals, understanding these dynamics is essential: club access and home value have become inseparable in the evolving landscape of private golf and social clubs.
