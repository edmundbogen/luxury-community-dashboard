Between 2020 and 2025, the United States witnessed the largest tax-motivated migration of wealth in modern history. And if you're reading this from New York, California, New Jersey, or Illinois, you're watching it happen in real time.

I'm not talking about a few hedge fund managers changing their mailing address. I'm talking about $196 billion in adjusted gross income flowing into Florida over five years. I'm talking about Palm Beach County alone capturing $7.2 billion—the highest of any county in America. I'm talking about a fundamental reshaping of where wealthy Americans choose to live, work, and build their legacy.

This isn't speculation. This is IRS data. Census Bureau migration reports. MLS transaction records. And as someone who made the move from New York to South Florida myself—and who now helps high-net-worth families navigate this transition daily at Douglas Elliman—I can tell you the migration isn't slowing down. It's accelerating.

Here's what's really happening, why it matters, and what it means if you're considering making the move in 2026.

The IRS data for tax year 2021-2022 alone shows Florida gained 125,551 net filers and $36 billion in adjusted gross income in a single year. Meanwhile, California lost $23.8 billion and New York lost $14.1 billion.

But here's what makes Palm Beach County exceptional: While Miami-Dade captured $4.8 billion, Palm Beach County took the crown at $7.2 billion—more than any other county in America. That's not beach tourism or seasonal visitors. That's permanent, tax-driven relocation of wealth.

  • $500,000 income: ~$60,000 saved

And it's not just income tax. Florida offers zero estate tax, generous homestead protections, and the Save Our Homes cap—which limits annual assessed value increases on primary residences to 3% or CPI, protecting long-term owners from runaway property taxes.

The Urgency Factor: Why 2026 Is Different

Here's what changed the calculus for many wealthy families: California and New York aren't just maintaining high taxes—they're proposing wealth taxes that would fundamentally alter the rules.

California's Billionaire Tax Act (2026 Ballot)

A 5% one-time excise tax on net worth exceeding $1 billion—applied retroactively to anyone who lived in California after January 1, 2025. This isn't income. This is a tax on wealth itself, including unrealized gains.

When this proposal hit the news, high-net-worth families started calling. Not to discuss whether to move, but when.

New York's Mark-to-Market Tax (Bill A3632)

Would require residents with net assets over $1 billion to recognize gains annually and pay tax over ten installments—treating assets as if "sold" at market value each year, even if they haven't sold anything.

These proposals haven't passed yet. But the signal they send is unmistakable: high-tax states are actively exploring ways to tax wealth, not just income. And once you establish domicile elsewhere, those laws can't follow you.

"You can't isolate yourself from the 49 other states. You've gotta be pragmatic about it."

Who's Moving and Where They're Going

The New York-to-Florida corridor dominates migration volume. Between 2018 and 2022, over 125,000 New Yorkers moved to Florida, bringing $14 billion in adjusted gross income. These weren't retirees downsizing. The average income of relocating New Yorkers was $182,895, with top-bracket households averaging $713,310 in AGI.

And 28.8% of all New Yorkers leaving the state chose Florida as their destination—by far the top choice.

California's migration pattern is different. While fewer Californians move to Florida in absolute numbers (8% of those leaving), the ones who do tend to be ultra-high-net-worth—often tech founders and investors fleeing wealth tax proposals.

What It Means for Buyers in 2026

If you're reading this and considering a move, here's the strategic reality:

Competition is real. You're not just competing with other buyers—you're competing with a structural shift in where America's wealth wants to live. Ultra-luxury inventory at St. Andrews, where I'm a resident, has dropped from 65 active listings six years ago to just 12 today. That scarcity isn't temporary.

Timing matters. Every year you delay costs real money. A household earning $1 million in New York and paying $120,000 annually in state and city income tax is forfeiting $120,000 per year by not establishing Florida domicile. Over five years, that's $600,000—enough to buy a second home.

Domicile establishment is non-negotiable. Florida's tax benefits only apply if you properly establish domicile: spending 183+ days in Florida, obtaining a Florida driver's license, registering to vote, filing a Declaration of Domicile, and updating financial accounts. High-tax states like New York and California audit aggressively, using cell phone data, credit card records, and social media to challenge claims. Work with a tax professional.

The Florida Advantage: Beyond Income Tax

  • Homestead Exemption: Up to $50,000 reduction on primary residence assessed value
  • Save Our Homes Cap: Annual assessed value increase capped at 3% or CPI
  • Portability: Transfer up to $500,000 in Save Our Homes benefit when moving within Florida
  • Creditor Protection: Constitutional protection for homestead property
  • No Estate Tax: Preserves multigenerational wealth transfer

Looking Ahead: What Happens Next

The Florida Demographic Estimating Conference projects the state will add 305,953 net new residents per year through 2030—equivalent to 838 people daily. That's a city larger than St. Petersburg arriving annually.

Florida is projected to reach 25+ million residents by 2030 and gain two additional congressional seats after the 2030 Census. Meanwhile, California is projected to lose 4-5 seats and New York 1-2 seats.

The MIAMI Realtors forecast for 2026 projects mortgage rates averaging 5.8% by year-end, with 4% modest appreciation in single-family homes and continued outperformance in the ultra-luxury segment. The National Association of Realtors forecasts a 14% increase in nationwide home sales in 2026, with Florida expected to exceed national averages.

"This isn't a temporary pandemic-driven trend. This is a structural realignment of where wealth chooses to reside when given the freedom to choose."

The Bottom Line

The great wealth migration isn't coming—it's here. $196 billion in adjusted gross income has already moved to Florida. Palm Beach County captured $7.2 billion, more than any other county in America. Home prices in Boca Raton are up 109% over the decade. Cash buyers represent half the market in West Palm Beach.

If you're earning over $500,000 annually in a high-tax state, you're effectively choosing to pay $50,000-$700,000 per year for the privilege of remaining there. That's not a judgment—it's math. For some, family ties, business requirements, or genuine love of place justify that cost. For many others, it doesn't.

What I tell clients is this: The decision to move isn't just financial. It's about quality of life, proximity to family, business needs, and personal values. But the financial component is real, measurable, and significant enough that ignoring it is a choice in itself.

The question isn't whether Florida makes sense for high-net-worth households. For most, it does. The question is whether you're ready to act on it—and whether you understand that the market you're entering is fundamentally more competitive than it was five years ago.

If you are ready, the time to move is now. Not because of fear, but because every year you wait is a year of savings foregone, appreciation missed, and opportunities passed by others who moved sooner.
