Introduction

Artificial Intelligence (AI) is rapidly transforming industries and labor markets around the world - and Florida is no exception. Recent advances in AI have sparked both excitement and concern in the Sunshine State. On one hand, AI promises new efficiencies and high-tech growth; on the other, it threatens to displace a significant number of jobs across various sectors.

Florida ranks among the top five states most exposed to AI-driven job losses.

For communities in Palm Beach County - including affluent enclaves like St. Andrews Country Club - understanding these trends is crucial. How might AI impact the local economy and employment by 2035, and what can be done to fortify our luxury market and workforce? This article explores expert forecasts, industry-specific risks, and potential solutions (from upskilling to universal basic income) to ensure Palm Beach County thrives in the AI era.

AI-Driven Job Displacement in Florida: Outlook to 2035

Major job displacement is a real possibility within the next decade. Multiple studies and expert analyses foresee AI and automation replacing a sizable share of jobs by 2035 if current trends continue. Globally, estimates range widely but are striking: the World Economic Forum projects 83 million jobs could be automated by 2027 worldwide, while Goldman Sachs research warns that up to 300 million jobs could be affected by AI in the coming years.

In the U.S., former presidential candidate Andrew Yang has pointed out that "44% of American jobs are either repetitive manual or repetitive cognitive" - and thus highly susceptible to automation. Yang cautions that if even half of those jobs are phased out, 30-40 million U.S. jobs could disappear within a decade, calling that prospect "devastating" for many communities.

Florida's workforce is particularly exposed. A recent analysis found 10.2% of all workers in Florida are in occupations at high risk of AI-driven automation, giving Florida the 4th largest share of vulnerable workers among U.S. states. In total, about 46% of Florida jobs have some risk from broader computerization.

South Florida is a hotspot: McKinsey Global Institute estimates that the Miami metropolitan area (Miami-Dade, Broward, and Palm Beach counties) could see 23% of its workforce displaced by automation by 2030 - roughly 761,000 jobs in South Florida alone.

What kinds of jobs are most at risk? According to the McKinsey study, the largest South Florida job losses by 2030 are expected in: office support roles (168,000 jobs), food service (106,000), and customer service and sales (100,000). Many administrative and clerical positions are already shrinking as businesses adopt digital tools. One South Florida business leader put it bluntly: "[These technologies] are replacing the employee."

Industries Most Vulnerable in Palm Beach County

Palm Beach County's economy is diverse, but certain key industries face higher AI disruption risks:

Office, Administrative & Customer Service

White-collar support jobs are among the most endangered by AI. This includes administrative assistants, clerks, bookkeepers, receptionists, and customer service representatives - many of whom perform repetitive tasks that AI or software automation can handle. In South Florida, office support roles are projected to see the largest declines due to automation.

Hospitality and Retail Services

Tourism is a pillar of Palm Beach County's economy, supporting over 66,000 local jobs from hotels and restaurants to shops and transportation. An analysis by Priority Software found that "cashier" is the single most vulnerable occupation in Florida, predicting nearly 21,000 cashier positions could be eliminated statewide by 2033 due to AI.

Finance and Legal Sectors

Palm Beach County has attracted an influx of financial firms and wealthy professionals. Paradoxically, AI is both a draw and a disrupter. AI is increasingly handling number-crunching, legal review, and even portfolio management - especially impacting accountants, auditors, paralegals, and loan officers.

Real Estate and Construction

The real estate industry is adopting predictive algorithms, AI-driven marketing, and smart contracts. While the luxury segment remains human-driven, automation may reduce hours and roles in lower-cost transactions. On the construction side, AI and robotics are being used for surveying, bricklaying, and machinery - meaning workers will need to reskill for tech-supported roles.

Agriculture

Palm Beach County is a top producer of sugarcane and vegetables. With AI-driven irrigation, drones, and autonomous tractors, farms will become more productive - but low-skill labor needs will drop sharply.

Health Care and Personal Services

AI is augmenting, not replacing, healthcare roles. Florida's aging population ensures strong demand for nurse practitioners and personal care aides, who benefit from AI tools but aren't replaced by them. Meanwhile, fitness, wellness, and luxury personal services will likely grow, as they require high-touch human interaction.

Fortifying the Local Economy in the AI Era

  • Invest in education and upskilling (especially AI-literate and STEM programs)
  • Grow high-tech and health sectors that complement AI
  • Encourage public-private partnerships to guide workforce transition
  • Strengthen support systems for displaced workers (training, housing, placement)

Is Universal Basic Income (UBI) Coming?

As automation rises, so does debate over UBI. Florida lawmakers have tried to ban government-funded guaranteed income pilots. But national voices - including Elon Musk and Andrew Yang - say UBI may be "necessary" to preserve stability if AI-driven job loss becomes widespread.

Palm Beach may not implement UBI anytime soon, but the conversation is clearly accelerating. If adopted, UBI could buoy consumer demand, support retraining, and help Florida's economy weather rapid transitions.

Conclusion

AI is here, and it's not slowing down. Whether it's displacing office staff, reshaping retail, or streamlining real estate, it will transform Florida's economy by 2035. Palm Beach County must lead with foresight - upskilling workers, welcoming new industries, and fortifying the middle class.

For the high-net-worth communities I work with, understanding this disruption isn't just good citizenship - it's good strategy.
