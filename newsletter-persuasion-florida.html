How to Get the People You Love to Say Yes to Florida

The Psychology, Data, and Strategy Behind Convincing a Reluctant Spouse—and Making Your Home the Place Grandkids Want to Visit

The hardest part of moving to Florida isn't finding the right home. It's getting everyone on board.

I'm writing this from two perspectives: as a luxury real estate professional at Douglas Elliman who helps clients navigate South Florida relocations daily, and as a St. Andrews Country Club resident who made the move from New York with my own family. That dual lens matters—because the question I hear most often isn't about square footage or HOA fees. It's this:

"How do I get my spouse to agree? And how do I make sure my kids and grandkids actually want to visit?"

These are emotional questions dressed up as logistical ones. And after years of guiding families through this transition, I've learned that the answers lie at the intersection of data and psychology—hard numbers that satisfy the analytical mind, and persuasion strategies that speak to the heart.

Let's cut through the noise and look at what actually works.

The Real Conversation You're Having

Here's what most people miss: A relocation decision is never just about geography. It's about identity, security, and belonging. When a spouse says "I'm not sure about Florida," they're rarely objecting to palm trees. They're expressing concerns about:

  • Leaving established friendships and community
  • Fear of the unknown (hurricanes, insurance, "starting over")
  • Worry that the move is permanent and irreversible
  • Concern about distance from children and grandchildren

Understanding these underlying concerns—rather than just countering with tax savings—is where successful persuasion begins.

"A meta-analysis of 30 studies found that couples who use inclusive pronouns—'we,' 'us,' 'our'—experience greater relationship satisfaction and are more likely to reach compromise. Changing 'you and I can work this out' to 'we can work this out' has a measurable impact on a partner's receptiveness."

The research is clear: Frame this as a joint adventure, not a unilateral decision. "Our new chapter" lands differently than "my retirement plan."

The Numbers That Matter: Making the Financial Case

For the analytically-minded spouse, the financial argument is compelling—but it needs to be presented honestly, including the trade-offs.

What You Save

  • No state income tax in Florida. For high earners, this alone can mean $20,000–$37,500+ in annual savings.
  • Lower property tax rates: Florida's average effective rate is approximately 0.80% vs. New York's 1.73%.
  • No estate tax and generous homestead protections.
  • Save Our Homes cap: Florida law limits annual increases in your primary residence's assessed value to 3% or the CPI—protecting long-term owners from runaway property taxes.
  • Corporate savings: Companies relocating from NYC to Boca Raton save an average of $7.5 million annually in operational costs—a 22.5% reduction.

What You Should Know

Honesty builds trust. Present the challenges upfront:

  • Insurance costs: Property insurance for a $300K home averages $5,409 annually, with flood-zone coverage adding more.
  • Property tax increases: Florida property taxes increased 47.5% from 2019–2024 as values rose.
  • Condo considerations: New reserve requirements mean many associations are issuing large special assessments.
  • Domicile requirements: To legally establish Florida residency and capture tax benefits, you need to spend more than 183 days in the state, obtain a Florida driver's license, register to vote, and file a declaration of domicile.

Legal Caution: A New York Tax Appeals Tribunal ruling held that a couple remained New York domiciled despite owning a Florida home—because they maintained primary ties (home, business, social connections) in New York. Secondary factors like driver's licenses carried little weight. States actively challenge domicile claims, examining home ownership, business involvement, time spent, personal items, and family connections. Work with a tax professional.

The Health and Lifestyle Case: Beyond the Numbers

Here's where it gets interesting—and where most relocation conversations fall short. The health benefits of Florida living aren't soft talking points. They're measurable.

Sunshine and Vitamin D

  • Adults need 600–2,000 IU of vitamin D daily
  • A few minutes of Florida sunlight can meet this requirement
  • Vitamin D deficiency is linked to bone loss, fatigue, and depression

Mental Health Benefits

  • Outdoor time reduces cortisol and blood pressure
  • Natural light regulates circadian rhythms and improves sleep
  • Warm climates increase serotonin production
  • Coastal living reduces seasonal affective disorder

The research on "blue space"—living near water—is particularly compelling. Studies show that people living on the coast report fewer depression symptoms, and older adults with access to water use fewer antidepressants. Ocean sounds and water activities reduce cortisol levels and encourage full-body exercise.

When I made the move from New York, I didn't anticipate how much the daily access to sunshine, golf, and waterfront living would affect my overall well-being. I can play 36 holes on a Tuesday morning and still handle business calls. That's not retirement—that's a better operating system for life.

The Safe Plan: Reducing Risk and Building Trust

Here's the strategy that converts the most reluctant spouses: Offer a trial period.

Research following 206 couples before and after relocation found that relocation stress decreases over time, financial rewards increase, and—importantly—open communication and planning help couples adapt successfully. The key insight: providing a clear plan reduces feelings of being trapped and gives the hesitant spouse a sense of control.

The Trial Year Approach:

  • Rent for 6–12 months before buying to explore neighborhoods and evaluate lifestyle fit
  • Maintain your northern residence initially (dual residency)
  • Commit to regular visits back to see friends and family
  • Set a decision point: "We'll evaluate together after one year"

This isn't about hedging your commitment—it's about demonstrating that you value your partner's concerns and that this is a shared decision with an exit ramp if needed.

Making Grandchildren Want to Visit: The Multigenerational Strategy

Now for the second question: How do you make your Florida home the place your kids and grandkids actually want to visit?

The data here is remarkable. The 2025 U.S. Family Travel Survey found:

  • 71% of grandparents took a multigenerational trip in the past three years
  • 44% plan to take one in the next three years
  • 60% of grandparents share costs; 37% pay for the entire trip
  • 57% of travelers want to establish new family traditions with grandparents
  • 45% of Baby Boomers would rather fund a big family trip than leave an inheritance

Beach vacations are the most popular multigenerational trips (54%), followed by visiting family and theme parks. The opportunity is clear: Florida is already where families want to gather.

"Surveys show that children are increasingly co-planners of family vacations. Giving grandchildren a voice in choosing activities—asking what they want to do when they visit—builds anticipation and makes your home their destination of choice."

The Psychology of Persuasion: What Actually Works

Let me share the evidence-based strategies that help couples reach alignment on major decisions like relocation.

Cialdini's Six Principles of Influence

Psychologist Robert Cialdini identified six universal principles that drive human decision-making. Here's how they apply:

1. Reciprocation

People feel obliged to give back to those who first give to them. Offering your spouse something they value—a commitment to visit family regularly, investing in their hobbies, maintaining friendships up north—triggers reciprocity. Studies show customers greeted with a small gift are 42% more likely to make a purchase.

2. Liking

We agree more readily with those we like. Validating your partner's concerns, being positive about the process, and complimenting their thoughtfulness fosters connection. (Waiters who say "good choice" increase tips by 13%.)

3. Social Proof

People look to what others like themselves are doing. Share stories of similar couples who relocated and thrived. Mention the growing number of multigenerational travelers. "Restaurant dishes labeled 'most popular' saw sales jump 13–20%."

4. Authority

Evidence from credible experts increases trust. Quote financial advisers on tax benefits, cite physicians on health advantages, reference club professionals about lifestyle quality. Stroke patients were 30% more likely to follow exercise regimens when provider credentials were displayed.

5. Scarcity

People value opportunities that seem limited. Highlighting constrained inventory in desirable communities or limited club memberships creates appropriate urgency. At St. Andrews, we currently have just 12 properties on the market—down from 65 six years ago.

6. Commitment and Consistency

Once people commit to a small step, they're more likely to follow through on larger ones. Ask your spouse to agree to a one-month trial stay, or to visit a club together, before discussing a permanent move.

Timing and Delivery

When you present the conversation matters as much as what you say. Research on persuasion shows that proposals presented at relaxed moments—after a pleasant dinner, during a Florida visit when the lifestyle is tangible—receive more favorable consideration than those raised during stressful times.

And remember: only about 7% of communication is verbal. Tone, facial expressions, and body language convey the rest. Maintaining open posture, eye contact, and a calm demeanor signals respect and reduces defensiveness.

What This Means for Different Audiences

For Those Trying to Convince a Spouse

Lead with lifestyle, not numbers. Describe daily routines—morning sun, golf or pickleball, working remotely with fast internet—before presenting financial data. Offer a trial period. Use inclusive language ("our adventure," "we could try"). Address concerns honestly, including challenges like insurance costs. And consider visiting during different seasons so your partner experiences Florida beyond vacation mode.

For Those Wanting Grandchildren to Visit

Create an anchor tradition—an annual family week (Presidents' Week, winter break, summer) that becomes non-negotiable family time. Offer to cover flights and coordinate logistics; surveys show grandparents who reduce barriers see more visits. Choose a home or club with age-appropriate amenities. And give grandchildren a voice in planning activities—kids who help plan are more invested in the trip.

For Current Florida Residents

If you're already here and want family to relocate nearby, the same principles apply. Share your experience authentically. Offer to host trial visits. Introduce them to your community and friends. And be patient—major life decisions take time.

The Bottom Line

Getting the people you love to say yes to Florida isn't about winning an argument. It's about building a shared vision.

The data supports the move: tax savings, health benefits, remote-work infrastructure, and a lifestyle that genuinely improves quality of life. The multigenerational travel trends show that families want to gather—and Florida, with its beaches, theme parks, and club amenities, is where they want to do it.

But data alone doesn't move hearts. What moves hearts is feeling heard, having concerns addressed honestly, being offered a safe path forward, and believing that this is a decision made together.

"From my perspective at St. Andrews, watching families reunite at the pool, seeing grandchildren learn tennis from club pros, hosting holiday gatherings in sunshine rather than snow—the return on this investment isn't measured in dollars saved. It's measured in memories made."

The question isn't whether Florida makes sense. For most high-net-worth families, it does. The question is whether you can present that case in a way that honors your partner's concerns, excites your grandchildren's imagination, and creates a future everyone wants to be part of.

That's the conversation worth having.
